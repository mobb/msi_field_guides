Kelps are large seaweeds belonging to the brown algae group. Giant kelp (Macrocystis pyrifera) is the most common type along the California coast. Kelp is one of the fastest growing species of algae on earth, growing up to 50 cm (2 feet) per day. Similar to land plants, kelp gets energy through photosynthesis, using sunlight to convert carbon dioxide and water to simple sugars. Kelp has BLADES (1), which are similar in shape to leaves. Blades absorb nutrients from the water and unlike land plants, kelp has GAS BLADDERS (2) that float the blades towards the surface to collect sunlight. These are attached to the STIPE (3), which is similar to a stem. The kelp FRONDS (4) create the canopy. A root-like structure called the HOLDFAST (5) anchors the kelp to the rocky seafloor.
